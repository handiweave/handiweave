Crafts of Himachal

विरासत-ए-हिमाचल

A Living Archive by Handiweave

Preamble from our Founder: At Handiweave, we don't just document crafts; we attempt to listen to the stories whispered by the loom, the chisel, and the needle. This page is our humble पुरज़ोर (effort) to map the soul of Himachal—one district, one craft at a time. What you see here is gathered through years of walking the mountain trails, sitting with उस्ताद कारीगर (master artisans), and understanding that each craft is not merely an object but a chapter in our collective memory.

This compilation serves as a formal reference for students, researchers, and cultural enthusiasts. It categorizes crafts by their primary material and technique while highlighting their Geographical Indication (GI) status—a legal recognition of their unique origin. We have consciously used Hindi terms for craft names and key concepts to retain authenticity while keeping descriptions accessible. Consider this your gateway to the tangible heritage of देवभूमि (Land of the Gods).

1. District Chamba

जिला चंबा

A. Chamba Rumal | चंबा रुमाल

Fibre & Embroidery GI Tagged

The Chamba Rumal is not merely an embroidered handkerchief; it is a narrative canvas where myth and needlework converge. Originating in the 17th-18th centuries under royal patronage, these rumals were often gifted during weddings and religious ceremonies. The artistry lies in the दो-रुखा टांका (double satin stitch), which creates an identical design on both sides of the fine muslin cloth. Themes are predominantly drawn from the रामायण, महाभारत, Bhagavata Purana, and scenes of courtly life.

Materials & Tools | सामग्री और उपकरण
  • Fabric: Hand-spun, fine मलमल (muslin) or khaddar, traditionally prepared by चीम्बा (weaver) communities.
  • Threads: Untwisted silk floss (पट), yielding a luminous sheen. Earlier, natural dyes from plants like मजीठ (madder) and हल्दी (turmeric) were used.
  • Process: The design is first drawn by a चित्रकार (painter) using charcoal. The embroidery is then executed with impeccable precision, often taking months for a single piece.
Cultural Significance & Present Status

Beyond its aesthetic value, the Rumal served as a medium of religious storytelling and a symbol of sophistication. By the late 20th century, the craft faced near extinction. Its revival is a success story of cultural activism. The Geographical Indication (GI) tag in 2008 legally protects its identity and origin. Today, institutions like the Bhuri Singh Museum and NGOs are training a new generation, ensuring the rumal evolves from a ceremonial object to a coveted art piece.

Note: The GI tag ensures only embroidery made in the Chamba district following traditional techniques can be labeled as 'Chamba Rumal'.

B. Chamba Leather Craft | चंबा चप्पल

Leather Craft

The Chamba Chappal is a masterpiece of functional elegance. Crafted from vegetable-tanned leather, its hallmark is the intricate embroidery on the strap, often using मोती (beads) and कलगी (threadwork). Traditionally worn by the elite and royalty, its design provides exceptional comfort for the mountainous terrain. The craft is a synergy between the चमार (cobbler) who prepares the sole and the दर्जी (embroiderer) who adorns it.

Technique and Decline

The sole is made from multiple layers of leather, hammered and stitched for durability. The upper strap is then embroidered with geometric or floral motifs. Sadly, the influx of cheap, synthetic footwear and a dwindling number of master craftsmen have pushed this craft to the brink. Today, only a handful of families in Chamba town continue this tradition, mostly on a custom-order basis.

C. Metal Craft & Ritual Objects | धातु शिल्प

Metalwork

Chamba's metalcraft is deeply sacrosanct, serving temple and home alike. The घंटा (temple bell) and थाल (ritual plate) are not just utensils but objects of पूजा (worship). Craftsmen, known as ठठेरा, work with copper, brass, and silver using techniques of casting, hammering, and repoussé. The famed चंबा थाल is often adorned with repoussé figures of deities.

This craft thrives due to continuous demand from temples and a growing market for heritage home decor.

D. Traditional Jewellery | पारंपरिक आभूषण

Jewellery Making

Pahari jewellery is characterized by its robust and symbolic design. Pieces like the चाँदबाली (crescent-shaped earrings), गुलोबन्द (choker), and शिखर (headpiece) are often made in heavy silver. Motifs include the कलश (pot), देवता (deity), and floral patterns. These are not mere ornaments but part of a woman's स्त्रिधन (personal wealth) and are worn during specific festivals and life-cycle rituals.

E. Chamba Painting | चंबा चित्रकला

Pahari Miniature

A distinct school within the Pahari miniature tradition, Chamba paintings are known for their lyrical quality, vibrant landscapes, and profound emotional expression. Unlike the Basohli style, Chamba uses a softer palette. The artists (नक़्क़ाश) worked on paper, cloth, and even wood, preparing their own pigments from minerals and plants. The subjects ranged from portraiture of Rajput rulers to elaborate राग-रागिनी series (musical modes).

Folk Instruments

Audio heritage of the hills

F. Traditional Musical Instruments | पारंपरिक वाद्य यंत्र

Wood & Hide

The soundscape of Chamba is defined by instruments crafted for specific rituals and festivals. The नगाड़ा (kettledrum) and रणसिंघा (war trumpet) are central to temple processions. The शहनाई (oboe) accompanies weddings. Made by specialized craftsmen, these instruments use local deodar wood, goat hide, and metal, tuned to a scale that resonates with the region's folk music.

This is a highly specialized, shrinking craft as electronic music replaces traditional ensembles.
Wood Carving

Architectural grandeur

G. Architectural Woodcraft | लकड़ी शिल्प

Wood Carving

The काठ-कुणी (timber-and-stone) style of architecture in Chamba is a marvel of earthquake-resistant design. The woodwork, however, is its soul. देवदार (deodar cedar) is intricately carved to create झरोखे (latticed windows), temple façades depicting gods and goddesses, and monumental doors. The सन्दूक (wedding chest) is another classic product, often carved with motifs of fertility and prosperity, serving as a crucial part of a bride's trousseau.

Basketry

Weaving for utility

H. Basketry & Weaving | चंबा की टोकरियाँ

Grass / Bamboo

This is the craft of everyday life. The conical किल्टा, carried on the back with a headstrap, is used by shepherds and farmers to transport goods. टोकरी (flat baskets) are used for storage and winnowing. Woven from रिंगाल (dwarf bamboo) and wild grasses, these items represent sustainable design long before the term was coined. Patterns and weaving techniques vary from valley to valley, encoding local identity.

2. District Kangra

जिला कांगड़ा

1. Gaddi Pattu | गद्दी पट्टू

Wool Weaving

The Gaddi tribe, primarily shepherds, weave the पट्टू on simple pit looms. This thick, coarse woolen fabric is their primary defense against the harsh winters of the Dhauladhar range. It is used to make the चोला (long coat) and दोरा (blanket). The weaving is often done by women during the long months in high-altitude pastures (ढ़ांड). The natural, undyed wool in shades of brown and white is its signature.

A vital part of the Gaddi pastoral identity, this craft remains strong within the community but is largely unknown outside.

2. Kangra Painting | कांगड़ा चित्रकला

Painting

The zenith of Pahari miniature art. Kangra paintings are celebrated for their ethereal beauty, lyrical landscapes, and the profound emotion (भाव) in the eyes of the figures, especially Radha and Krishna. The palette is derived from minerals and plants: green from malachite, blue from lapis lazuli, white from conch shells. The art flourished under the patronage of Maharaja Sansar Chand Katoch and represents a sublime fusion of poetry and visual art.

Technique & Legacy

Artists prepared their own paper from bamboo pulp or used old manuscripts. Brushes were made from squirrel hair. The Kangra style's defining feature is its idealised, naturalistic world—serene rivers, lush forests, and architectural details that mirror the region's own pavilions. While the royal patronage is gone, a dedicated community of artists in places like Nurpur and Andretta continues to keep this legacy alive, adapting it to contemporary themes.

3. Basohli Painting | बसोहली चित्रकला

Early Pahari Style

The progenitor of the Kangra style, Basohli paintings (17th century) are powerful and dramatic. They are characterized by bold, vibrant colors (vermillion red, lapis blue), stylized facial features with receding foreheads and large, expressive eyes, and the use of elaborate beetle-wing cases for jewelry. The रासमंजरी series is a famous example. This style is less about naturalism and more about symbolic intensity and direct emotional appeal.

4. Slate Craft | स्लेट शिल्प

Stone Craft

The slate mines of Dehra and Shahpur have supplied material for centuries. Beyond its primary use as a durable roofing material (पत्थर की छत), slate is carved into traditional writing tablets (तख्ती), blackboards, and now decorative items like coasters and plaques. The craft involves skilled splitting of the rock along its natural grain and then shaping it—a knowledge passed down through generations of पत्थर-कटाई (stone-cutters) communities.

Appliqué Art

Silk and devotion

5. Drass Dub Ma (Appliqué Thangka) | ड्रास डुब मा

Textile Art

A unique craft of the Tibetan diaspora in McLeod Ganj and Dharamshala. Unlike painted Thangkas, these are created by meticulously cutting and stitching pieces of silk and brocade onto a canvas to form intricate religious images of Buddhas, deities, and mandalas. The result is a radiant, textured masterpiece used in monasteries and for personal meditation. The craft requires deep religious knowledge alongside tailoring skills.

Metal Embossing

The dance of hammer and chisel

6. Thakkat Ka Kaam (Repoussé) | ठक्कट का काम

Sheet Metal Work

This is the art of raising a design from the reverse side of a metal sheet (usually copper or brass) using hammers and punches. In Kangra, it is traditionally used for creating temple doors, ritual masks (मुखौटे), and vessels. The craftsman works from the back, skillfully hammering to create a bas-relief image on the front, which is then often finely chased and detailed. The sound of the tapping hammer gives the craft its onomatopoeic name.

7. Thangka Painting | थांका चित्रकला

Religious Painting

A sacred art form integral to Tibetan Buddhism, now practiced in Kangra's monastic communities. Thangkas are scroll paintings depicting deities, mandalas, or scenes from the Buddha's life, used as meditation aids. The process is ritualistic: preparing the canvas with gesso, sketching the iconographically precise grid, applying mineral pigments mixed with glue, and finally, consecrating the painting. The Dharamshala region is a vital center for preserving this spiritual craft.

Tibetan Woodwork

Carving for the divine

8. Woodwork of Dharamshala | धर्मशाला की लकड़ी शिल्प

Wood Carving

A beautiful synthesis of Himachali and Tibetan aesthetics. Local deodar and walnut wood are carved into monastic furniture like चोकसी (altar tables), prayer wheels, and window frames for temples and homes. Motifs blend local floral patterns with Tibetan Buddhist symbols like the endless knot (दपे) and lotus. This craft supports both the local carpentry tradition and the needs of the Tibetan refugee community.

9. Clay Craft of Kangra | कांगड़ा की मिट्टी शिल्प

Pottery

The कुम्हार (potter) communities, using the distinctive red and black clay of the region, create both utilitarian and ritual objects. The घड़ा (water pot) for cool storage, the कुल्हड़ (clay cup) for tea, and the दीया (lamp) for Diwali are common. The craft is wheel-based, with firing done in traditional open-pit kilns. While under pressure from plastic and metal, a revival for artistic and eco-friendly tableware is emerging.

10. Bahloti & Charoti Metal Casting | बहलोटी धातु शिल्प

Metal Casting

An ancient form of metal casting using the lost-wax (मोम ढला) technique, practiced by specific artisan clans. Intricate wax models are made, coated in clay, fired (melting the wax), and molten brass or bronze is poured in. This creates exquisite ritual objects like bells (घंटा), oil lamps (दीपदान), and statues. The skill lies in designing the wax model to account for metal flow and shrinkage.

Rope Making

Twisting nature's fibers

11. Traditional Rope-Making | रस्सी निर्माण

Fibre Craft

A ubiquitous but under-documented craft. Fibers from the ब्यूल tree bark, hemp, or even paddy husk are twisted together by hand or using simple tools to create ropes of various thicknesses. These ropes (रस्सी) are essential for agriculture (tying cattle), domestic use (charpai beds), and construction. It represents a profound knowledge of local plant properties and sustainable resource use.

12. Bamboo & Basketry | बांस शिल्प

Bamboo Work

The abundant bamboo groves of the lower hills are harvested sustainably to create a wide range of products. From the sturdy किल्टा and टोकरी to furniture, mats, and even fishing traps, bamboo craft is versatile. The split bamboo is woven using techniques that ensure strength and flexibility. This craft is eco-friendly, renewable, and deeply integrated into rural Himachali life.

3. District Kullu

जिला कुल्लू

1. Kullu Shawl | कुल्लू शॉल

Handloom GI Tagged

The iconic Kullu Shawl, with its brilliant contrasting borders (पल्लू), is a symbol of the valley. Woven on pit looms, it uses a twill weave. The borders feature geometric patterns like अखर (chessboard), चक्र (wheel), and त्रिकोण (triangle), woven with acrylic or wool yarn. Traditionally made from local sheep wool, it is now also made from merino, pashmina, and angora. The GI tag (2005) protects its authenticity, mandating specific weaving techniques and design origins within the Kullu district.

The GI Protection

The GI tag for 'Kullu Shawl' is a landmark. It legally defines the product and its area of production, preventing imitation shawls from being sold under the Kullu name. This has helped organize the weaver community into cooperatives, ensured better wages, and maintained quality standards. It is a prime example of how legal recognition can empower traditional crafts economically.

2. Hand Knitting | हाथ बुनाई

Textile Craft

More than a craft, knitting is a necessary domestic skill for surviving Kullu winters. Women in villages like Sainj and Banjar knit heavy sweaters (स्वेटर), socks (मोजे), caps (टोपी), and gloves using four or five needles. Patterns are often traditional and passed down through generations, creating distinctive local styles. This is a vibrant, living craft that forms a crucial part of the rural economy and personal wardrobes.

3. Malana Cap Embroidery | मलाणा टोपी

Embroidery

The Malana Cap is a potent symbol of identity for the reclusive Malana village community. Made from handwoven woolen fabric, it is embroidered with brightly colored threads in distinct zigzag and diamond patterns. Wearing it is a statement of belonging to Malana's ancient culture. The craft is almost exclusively practiced within the village, making it a rare and culturally specific art form.

A fascinating example of craft as a marker of ethnic and social identity.

4. Pullan (Wool-Felted Slippers) | पुल्लन

Footwear / Felting

An ingenious adaptation to the cold, wet climate. पुल्लन are made by felting sheep wool together with dried भोजपत्र grass. The mixture is pounded and shaped while wet, creating a dense, water-resistant, and warm slipper. Once common footwear for shepherds, it is now a critically endangered craft, made only by a few elders for ceremonial purposes or as curiosities. It represents a sustainable, location-specific material innovation.

Grass Mat Weaving

Patterns from the pasture

5. Mandri (Grass Mat) | मंडड़ी

Grass Weaving

Woven from मूंज grass harvested from riverbanks, the मंडड़ी was a versatile household item—used as a sitting mat, a bed, or a surface for drying grains. The weaving creates beautiful, durable patterns. With the advent of plastic mats and changed lifestyles, this craft has virtually disappeared, remembered only by the oldest generation. It speaks of a time when every household resource was fully utilized.

6. Woodcraft (Banauta) & Kath-Kuni Architecture | काष्ठ कला

Architectural Craft

Kullu's wooden architecture is world-renowned. The काठ-कुणी style uses interlocking deodar logs and stone without nails, creating structures resilient to earthquakes. The बनौता (woodcarver) then embellishes this framework with spectacular carvings on doors, windows, and pillars, depicting deities, mythical animals, and floral motifs. The देव मंजी (palanquin) for carrying village gods during festivals is the pinnacle of this mobile woodcraft.

7. Ritual Metal Craft (Mohra & Musical Instruments) | मोहरा और वाद्य यंत्र

Ritual Metalwork

The मोहरा is a repoussé metal mask representing a local deity, mounted on a wooden frame. These are central to temple rituals and processions. Alongside, craftsmen forge large ritual trumpets like the करनाल and नरसिंघा from copper or brass sheets. The making of these objects is governed by religious prescriptions and is considered a sacred duty, often performed by specific families.

8. Traditional Jewellery Making | आभूषण शिल्प

Jewellery

Kullu jewellery is distinct and heavy. Women wear the पौंची (wide silver bracelet), चन्दनहार (multi-layered necklace), and टोड़ा (nose ring). The गोखरू (toe ring) is also common. Designs are bold and geometric, meant to be seen and to signify social status. Silversmiths in Kullu town and Naggar continue this trade, though demand for everyday wear has decreased in favor of festival and wedding use.

9. Stone Craft | पत्थर शिल्प

Stone Carving

Evident in the ancient stone temples of Bajaura, Naggar, and Jagatsukh, Kullu's stonecraft uses locally quarried grey schist and slate. Craftsmen carve intricate figures of gods, goddesses, and decorative motifs directly into stone slabs and pillars. While not a vibrant contemporary craft, these historical monuments stand as a testament to the high level of skill achieved by master पत्थर-तराश (stone carvers) in the past.

10. Sela (Goat Hair Coat) | सेला

Tribal Textile Critically Endangered

The सेला is a lifesaver for shepherds in the high-altitude valleys of Banjar and Lug. Woven entirely from coarse goat hair, it is waterproof, windproof, and incredibly warm. The hair is hand-twisted into yarn and woven on a simple loom. No modern fabric matches its utility for transhumant pastoral life. However, with changing lifestyles and fewer shepherds, the knowledge of making Sela is held by only a handful of elderly individuals, making it a craft on the verge of permanent loss.

Handiweave Note: This craft is a high priority for documentation and potential revival efforts.

4. District Lahaul & Spiti

जिला लाहौल-स्पीति

1. Lahauli Socks & Gloves | लाहौली मोजे

Knitting GI Tagged

These are not ordinary socks; they are engineered for survival in sub-zero temperatures. Hand-knitted from local sheep and yak wool blend, they feature dense, double-layered knitting and intricate geometric patterns that are often clan-specific. The GI tag recognizes their unique origin and quality. Products include the पट्टू (socks), चोलु (gloves), and थुडुंग (cap), forming a complete thermal protection suite.

2. Kharkhana (Carpet Weaving) | खरखाना

Textile Weaving

Introduced by Tibetan refugees, carpet weaving on vertical looms (ताना) has become a significant craft. Using the Tibetan knot, weavers create dense, plush carpets and rugs. Motifs are deeply symbolic: the दुक (eternal knot), द्रुक (dragon), snow lions, and floral scrolls. Natural dyes from plants and minerals were traditionally used, giving subdued, elegant colors. This craft provides vital livelihood, especially for women in the region.

3. Thangka & Appliqué Art | थंका चित्रकला

Religious Art

In the monastic heartland of Spiti and Lahaul, Thangka painting is a spiritual discipline. Monks and trained artists spend years learning the precise iconometry. The process is meditative, from preparing the canvas with lime to applying gold leaf. Monasteries like Key, Tabo, and Dhankar house ancient and contemporary Thangkas, serving as both objects of worship and repositories of artistic knowledge. Appliqué Thangkas (गोस-དབྱིབས) are also made here.

Monastic Woodwork

Carving for enlightenment

4. Monastic Woodwork & Carving | लकड़ी का काम

Wood Carving

The woodwork in Spiti's monasteries is austerely beautiful. Using scarce, locally sourced poplar or willow, craftsmen create massive, painted entrance doors, intricately carved and painted pillars, altar tables (चोकसी), and cabinets for sacred texts. The motifs are purely Buddhist—lotuses, clouds, the eight auspicious symbols. The carving is often painted in vibrant mineral pigments, creating a stark contrast against the barren landscape.

Ritual Metalwork

Sound and symbolism

5. Religious Metal Craft | धार्मिक धातु शिल्प

Metal Casting

Buddhist ritual requires specific metal objects. Craftsmen, often connected to monasteries, cast गर-घंट (hand bells), दिलबु (butter lamps), prayer wheels, and small votive statues using the lost-wax method. The most skilled create large bronze statues of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas. The craft blends technical skill with religious devotion, as the objects are considered embodiments of the divine, not mere artifacts.

6. Clay & Mud Crafts | मिट्टी शिल्प

Sculpture

The large, polychrome clay statues inside the Tabo monastery are among the finest examples of Indo-Tibetan sculptural art. Made from local mud mixed with straw and other binders, they are modeled over a wooden armature. The craft extends to making तोर्मा—ritual sculptures of butter and barley flour used in ceremonies. This ephemeral craft underscores the Buddhist philosophical concept of impermanence.

Bone & Horn Craft

Utilizing every resource

7. Bone & Horn Craft | अस्थि शिल्प

Organic Material

In a resource-scarce environment, nothing is wasted. Yak horn and bone are carved and polished to create functional and decorative items: beads for malas, amulet cases (गऊ), buttons, and even ladles. The crafting process is simple but requires patience. This craft highlights a deep ethic of sustainability and respect for the animal, turning by-products into objects of beauty and utility.

Endangered Crafts

Fading knowledge

8. Endangered & Lost Crafts | लुप्तप्राय शिल्प

Several crafts have nearly vanished: Spiti Boots made from untanned hide with woolen interiors; Namlo (wool rope) weaving for tying loads; and the creation of Painted Manuscript Covers for religious texts. These were highly specialized skills adapted to an isolated, self-sufficient lifestyle. Their decline marks a significant shift in the cultural and material life of the cold desert.

5. District Mandi

जिला मंडी

Clay, Stone & Weaving Traditions | मंडी के शिल्प

Mandi, known as 'चोटी काशी', has a diverse craft heritage rooted in its temple culture and fertile valleys. The potters of Gohar are famed for large fermentation jars (मडकी) for local brews. The stone carvers have created the iconic black stone idols in Mandi's many Shiv temples. In the Seraj valley, women weave sturdy पट्टू shawls on looms, similar to but distinct from Kullu styles, using local wool.

These crafts, while less commercialized, are integral to the local ritual and domestic economy.

Temple Architecture & Metalwork | मंदिर वास्तुकला और धातु शिल्प

Mandi's wooden देवता घर (deity houses) showcase exquisite woodcarving, often painted in bright colors. In Rewalsar, a multi-faith pilgrimage site, one finds a unique blend of Hindu and Buddhist metalcraft—brass bells, votive lamps, and ritual objects supplied to monasteries and temples alike. This syncretic craft environment is a special feature of Mandi district.

6. District Kinnaur

जिला किन्नौर

1. Kinnauri Weaving | किन्नौरी शॉल

Handloom

Kinnauri shawls are among the most sophisticated weaves of the Himalayas. Characterized by extra-weft patterning, they feature intricate geometric borders (पल्लू) woven with meticulous care. Motifs include the चारभुज (swastika), देव-मूर्ति (deity figures), and दरख्त (tree of life). Woven on narrow pit looms, they are traditionally made for personal use and are a key component of Kinnauri ethnic dress for both men and women.

2. Ritual Ropes (Dori) | देव रस्सियाँ

Sacred Fibre Craft

In Kinnaur, rope-making is elevated to a sacred act. Specially braided ropes are made from the inner bark of the ब्यूल tree, yak hair, or hemp for the annual फुलेच festival. These ropes are used to tie the palanquins of local deities during their ceremonial processions. The strength of the rope is believed to symbolize the unbreakable bond between the deity and the community. The knowledge of making these specific ropes is held by a few.

3. Silver Jewellery | चांदी के आभूषण

Jewellery

Kinnauri silverwork is monumental. Women wear substantial pieces: the चक्कर (broad headband), बुलाक (large nose ring), and the डिग्रा (elaborate shawl pin). The silver is often oxidized to highlight the intricate repoussé and filigree work. This jewellery represents family wealth and is a crucial part of the bride's trousseau. The designs are distinct from other Pahari styles, reflecting Kinnaur's unique cultural position.

7. District Hamirpur

जिला हमीरपुर

Bamboo, Grass & Ironware | हमीरपुर के शिल्प

Hamirpur's crafts are utilitarian and robust. Bamboo and Grass are woven into दाड़ी (sleeping mats) and storage baskets by the community. Traditional Ironware involves the local blacksmith (लोहार) forging agricultural tools like sickles (दरांती), axes, and nails using time-honored techniques. These crafts support the agrarian lifestyle but face competition from factory-made goods.

Woodcraft & Domestic Knitting | लकड़ी और बुनाई

Woodcraft focuses on making agricultural implements (ploughs, yokes) and simple furniture. Domestic Knitting of woolens is widespread, producing serviceable sweaters and caps for family use rather than commercial sale. These crafts represent the self-reliant, household-level production that forms the backbone of rural economies in the lower hills.

8. District Una

जिला ऊना

Khajur (Date Palm Leaf) Craft | खजूर की चटाई

Leaf Weaving

A specialty of the warmer lowlands of Una, this craft uses dried leaves of the date palm tree. Woven into mats (चटाई), fans, and hats, these products are naturally cooling and sustainable. The weaving technique is simple but effective, creating tight, durable surfaces. This craft demonstrates the adaptation of material use to local flora and climate, providing an eco-friendly alternative to plastic mats.

Supplementary Crafts | अन्य शिल्प

Like neighboring districts, Una also has a tradition of Rope Making from natural fibers and basic Pottery for domestic use. These crafts, while not highly elaborate, are essential to daily life and represent a continuity of traditional knowledge in the Shivalik belt.

9. District Bilaspur

जिला बिलासपुर

Bilaspur's Craft Heritage | बिलासपुर के शिल्प

Handloom (Pattu): Weaving of simple, warm पट्टू shawls and blankets on pit looms, often with minimalistic geometric borders.

Pottery: Distinctive black and red pottery from Ghumarwin, made from locally sourced clay and fired in a way that creates the two-tone effect. Used for cooking and storage.

Charpai Weaving: The art of weaving the webbing for the traditional cot (चारपाई) using natural fiber ropes, ensuring the perfect tension and comfort.

Bilaspur's crafts are characterized by their straightforward functionality and deep integration into household routines.

10. District Solan

जिला सोलन

Pine Needle Craft | चीड़ सुई शिल्प

Eco Craft / Weaving

An innovative and sustainable craft that has emerged in the pine-forested areas around Solan, particularly among women's self-help groups. Fallen pine needles are collected, cleaned, and woven into beautiful baskets, trays, coasters, and decorative items. The craft solves the problem of flammable forest litter while creating livelihood opportunities. It represents a modern evolution of traditional weaving knowledge applied to a new, ecological context.

A shining example of how traditional craft sensibilities can address contemporary environmental and economic challenges.

Other Local Crafts | स्थानीय शिल्प

Solan, being an urban and industrial hub, has seen many traditional crafts diminish. However, remnants of woodturning for domestic utensils and knitting persist in villages. The district's craft story is one of adaptation, where the innovative Pine Needle Craft stands out as a successful model for the future.

11. District Sirmour

जिला सिरमौर

Sirmour's Traditional Crafts | सिरमौर के शिल्प

Ringal (Dwarf Bamboo) Craft: Expert weaving of fine baskets, the iconic rain shield (छतरी), and containers from Ringal bamboo. This requires great skill due to the thin, flexible nature of the material.

Endangered Crafts: The district was once known for the Sela (goat hair coat) and the Chharpat (grass raincoat), both brilliant adaptations to the rainy climate. These are now rarely made, preserved only in the memory of elders and museum collections.

Sirmour's crafts speak of a life closely attuned to the monsoon ecology of the outer Himalayas.

12. District Shimla

जिला शिमला

Shimla's Craft Mosaic | शिमला के शिल्प

As the state capital, Shimla district is a confluence of influences from all over Himachal, reflected in its crafts.

  • Bushahri Topi: The elegant velvet-banded cap from Rohru/Chirgaon, a symbol of Pahari gentility.
  • Wood Carving: The intricate temple architecture and domestic woodwork of Jubbal, Rohru, and Chopal are renowned.
  • Doll Making: Traditional cloth dolls dressed in authentic Pahari costumes, often made as toys or souvenirs.
  • Fruit Box Making: A semi-industrial craft from apple-growing areas, involving the construction of sturdy wooden crates for transporting fruit—a craft born from the horticultural economy.
Shimla's crafts thus range from the deeply traditional to those shaped by modern economic activities.
